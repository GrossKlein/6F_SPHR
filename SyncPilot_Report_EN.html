SyncPilot SE

The Board That Sets Every Price, the Lawyer Who Served Both Sides, and the €250 Million Pitch Built on a €5.6 Million Signal

Chapter One

A €250 Million Pitch, Eighteen Months After the Company’s Own Transaction Record Implied €5.6 Million

The illusion: the Pictet financing validates SyncPilot’s value and confirms independent institutional assessment.

SyncPilot SE supplies digital consultation software to the Bundesagentur für Arbeit, Berlin’s Senatskanzlei, and eleven regional AOK health insurance offices. Eighty-four percent of its projected €44 million in revenue comes from organisations funded by German taxpayers. In late 2024, Pictet Asset Management’s European Direct Lending fund extended debt financing to the company. Hogan Lovells advised, deploying at least ten professionals across Munich, Frankfurt, Hamburg, and Düsseldorf. The implied valuation: €250 million.

Eighteen months before that financing closed, 127,885 shares in SyncPilot changed hands at €1.00 each — implying a company worth €5.6 million. The board that approved the €1.00 price and the board that authorised the €250 million pitch are the same board. Every price in SyncPilot’s history was set by that board. No independent party has ever valued this company.

This chapter documents what a credit committee assessing SyncPilot should have been shown, and what the transaction record suggests they may not have seen.

The Same Board That Set the €1.00 Share Price Pitched a €250 Million Valuation Eighteen Months Later

The arithmetic cannot be reconciled without an explanation that the record does not contain.

In November 2023, Franz-Xaver Bleicher, SyncPilot’s sole managing director, presented investors with a €250 million valuation on approximately €12 million in annual recurring revenue. The implied revenue multiple: 37×. Eight weeks earlier, the company’s own capital history had produced a market signal of €5.6 million, when 127,885 shares changed hands at €1.00 — a price the same board set. There was no restructuring, no recapitalisation, and no disclosed explanation for the gap between those two numbers.

The range a credit committee might apply to a European mid-market SaaS business at €12 million in revenue — before discounting for governance risk — is approximately 5–10× revenue, or €60–120 million. The €250 million pitch is nearly three times the top of that range. The Perseus share-price signal is forty-five times lower.

Pictet provided the financing. Hogan Lovells, with ten professionals across four cities, conducted due diligence. This was not a light-touch process. If the three categories of information described in this chapter were absent from the credit file despite that level of professional engagement, the implication is either that the information was not produced by SyncPilot — or not sought.

Three Categories of Information That Would Have Changed the Credit Committee’s Picture

A standard credit memorandum for a mid-market direct lending transaction would typically contain the company’s full capital history — including all share issuances and price movements — any disclosed litigation or contingent liability, and the identity, background, and governance role of all controlling parties. Three items in SyncPilot’s record are each material to that assessment.

The Perseus pricing anomaly. In March 2023, 127,885 SyncPilot shares changed hands at €1.00 — a 95% discount to both the preceding and subsequent transactions involving the same buyer. The buyer, H.G. Perseus GmbH StBG, is controlled by Martin Hunstein and Diethard Erik Goerg, both defendants in one of Germany’s largest tax fraud prosecutions. A credit committee assessing SyncPilot’s creditworthiness without seeing this transaction — or without an explanation of it — was working from an incomplete capital history. The €250 million valuation pitch was made into a market that had, eighteen months earlier, priced the same company at €5.6 million. Whether and how that context was disclosed to Pictet is a question the credit file can answer.

The LVS contingent liability. Between April and August 2021, LVS GmbH transferred €6.15 million to SyncPilot under a cooperation and call option agreement. No shares were ever issued. No repayment has been recorded. The agreement, which entitled LVS to 375,000 shares in the holding structure, was never notarised — a requirement under German law for share option contracts of this kind. If the contract is void on this basis, SyncPilot holds €6.15 million received on a defective legal instrument, and its liability may be reconfigured under §812 BGB (unjust enrichment). LVS is currently in litigation. Any lender evaluating SyncPilot’s debt capacity without sight of this liability was working from an incomplete balance sheet.

The sole-director control structure. Franz-Xaver Bleicher serves as Geschäftsführer of SyncPilot and of at least six related entities. He controls 70% of BOSYNC Beteiligungs UG, SyncPilot’s single largest shareholder. He is the only person with operational authority over the company’s capital allocation. Every share price, every capital event, every distribution decision in SyncPilot’s history ran through him. Pictet’s stated approach to direct lending includes operational engagement with portfolio companies. In practice, every governance mechanism at SyncPilot routes through a single individual — the same individual who approved the March 2023 insider discount, the same individual who received LVS’s €6.15 million and allocated it without issuing shares.

Hogan Lovells Deployed Ten Professionals. The Question Is What They Were Shown.

The applicable German framework is direct. §264a StGB (Kapitalanlagebetrug) criminalises material misrepresentation or concealment in materials used to solicit investment. Prospekthaftung creates civil liability where investment decisions rest on an inaccurate picture of a company’s financial condition. Whether those thresholds are crossed here depends on what Pictet’s credit file contains: the treatment of the Perseus share history, the disclosure or non-disclosure of the LVS liability, and the representation of the governance structure.

We are not asserting that Pictet was deceived. We are asserting that three categories of material information — each documented in public records, none requiring a confidential source to find — were each relevant to a lender’s risk assessment of SyncPilot. The Handelsregister is public. The LVS dispute is in litigation. The Perseus transaction is timestamped. Whether they were disclosed is, therefore, a question about what Pictet chose to ask — or what SyncPilot chose to provide.

The credit file exists. It contains Pictet’s answer to the three questions this chapter raises. The absence of a voluntary disclosure on this point is itself a signal.

Chapter Two

The Structure That Looks Like Governance Is Designed to Concentrate Control

The illusion: 31 investors, 26 holding entities, and an SAP partnership signal an institutional-grade, well-governed business.

SyncPilot SE (Societas Europaea, registered Augsburg) develops software for guided online consultations — co-browsing, video conferencing, and screen-sharing between agents and customers. Its target is regulated industries where compliance demands that the customer sees exactly what the agent sees in real time. The business is predominantly German public sector: the Bundesagentur für Arbeit, eleven AOK regional offices, the Berlin Senatskanzlei, the AKDB Bavaria, and social insurance bodies account for 84.3% of €44.2 million in projected client revenue. The five largest clients alone — Bundesagentur für Arbeit, ekom21, Senatskanzlei Berlin, ITSG, and AKDB Bayern — account for 41% of total projected revenue. Enterprise names (Volkswagen, Vodafone, AXA, E.ON) appear on the client list; together they represent less than 15% of revenue.

The surface architecture reads as institutional grade: 31 ultimate beneficial owners, 26 holding entities, shareholders in Germany, Malta, Luxembourg, Switzerland, and Liechtenstein, a Silver SAP partnership, institutional debt financing. The documents behind that architecture tell a different story.

The Structure Doesn’t Distribute Power — It Distributes Opacity

One hundred percent of SyncPilot’s equity routes through 26 intermediate holding entities across six jurisdictions. No share has ever changed hands on a public exchange. No independent valuation has ever been commissioned. The structure does not distribute governance — it distributes the distance between the company’s activities and external scrutiny.

Franz-Xaver Bleicher (born 9 September 1972, Augsburg) founded SyncPilot and has served as its sole managing director since formation. He held 50.9% of the company at incorporation in September 2020. By August 2025, his effective stake had declined to 16.48% — but he remains the single largest beneficial owner and the only person with operational authority over the company. His stake is held entirely through BOSYNC Beteiligungs UG, of which he is managing director and 70% owner.

That dilution — from majority founder to largest single minority holder — looks, in isolation, like the standard trajectory of a scaling company bringing in investors. It is not. The capital events that reduced Bleicher’s nominal stake were approved by the board he controls, at prices the board he controls set. The mechanism that diluted his percentage is the same mechanism that produced the 95% insider discount documented in Chapter Three. The two facts are not separable.

The next two largest beneficial owners — Dr. Ingo Krocke (8.65%) via Attegia Holding Ltd. in Malta, and Harald Steiner (8.58%) via AURIGA Capital AG in Switzerland — hold their stakes without those names appearing directly in SyncPilot’s share register. Together, the top three individuals hold 33.71% of the company through entities in three jurisdictions. The remaining 28 UBOs hold the balance through an additional 23 entities.

No external party can verify, from the public record, whether capital decisions in this company were made in the collective interest of its 31 shareholders or in the interest of the one person who controls the board. That opacity is structural, not incidental.

Bleicher additionally sits at the centre of a network encompassing 67 persons, 60 entities, and approximately 150 documented connections. Every operating entity with revenue exposure connects to him. The network is not evidence of wrongdoing; it is evidence of the degree to which governance depends on a single point of trust.

Every Price in the Company’s History Was Set by One Man

SyncPilot has conducted ten capital events between September 2020 and August 2025. The share price per event — set by the board, never by an independent appraiser — ranged from €1.00 to €23.64. The company’s projected annual recurring revenue climbed from €6.9 million in January 2024 to €25.5 million by December 2025 — a 268% increase on paper, reported by the same board that set those prices.

The €128 million paper valuation that SyncPilot currently carries is the board’s own assessment. It has never been tested against an independent view of the company’s assets, revenue quality, or liability position. No share has ever traded on a public exchange at any price.

The SAP Badge Is a Revenue Mechanism. It Has Not Been Verified.

SyncPilot holds a Silver partnership with SAP, downgraded from Gold. When a German public-sector procurement office evaluates a SyncPilot bid and sees “SAP Silver Partner,” the badge functions as an endorsement — it implies that SAP has screened the company and found it creditable. German public-sector procurement decisions worth six figures and above are made in part on that basis. The badge is not decorative; it is a revenue mechanism.

SAP’s Partner Code of Conduct requires that all work performed by partners be in full compliance with applicable laws, rules, and regulations. SyncPilot’s governance record — two shareholders prosecuted in a €1 billion tax fraud case, a €6.15 million investor dispute, no independent share valuation, a 95% insider discount — raises the question of whether SAP’s partner compliance process ever reviewed the facts it was designed to review. When SyncPilot was downgraded from Gold to Silver, the basis for that downgrade — administrative or compliance-driven — has not been disclosed.

The SAP badge underwrites 84% of the revenue base. The revenue base backs the Pictet loan. If SAP were to conduct a compliance review and suspend the partnership, the revenue base underpinning SyncPilot’s debt service would be materially impaired. That is not a journalistic question. It is a credit risk.

A lender assessing SyncPilot’s creditworthiness would ordinarily rely on an independent valuation, audited accounts, and a transparent ownership chain. None of the three is available here. The valuation is the board’s own assessment. The ownership chain runs through 26 entities in six jurisdictions. The audit trail of capital allocation is, by design, exclusively internal.

Chapter Three

Same Board. Same Buyer. Ninety-Five Days. Ninety-Five Percent. No Explanation in the Record.

The illusion: SyncPilot’s capital raises were arm’s-length transactions that reflected the company’s value.

On 9 December 2022, H.G. Perseus GmbH StBG acquired 158,208 shares of SyncPilot at €18.96 per share, paying €3,000,004. The board set the implied company valuation at €100 million.

Ninety-five days later, on 14 March 2023, the same entity acquired 127,885 additional shares at €1.00 per share — paying €127,885. The implied valuation fell to €5.6 million. A 95% discount, from the same board, to the same buyer.

Two months after that, on 11 May 2023, a third party — Tiven GmbH — acquired shares at €20.56 per share, implying a valuation of approximately €112 million. On 5 June 2023, Perseus itself bought 16,211 more shares at €20.56.

There Is No Business Explanation in the Record

Between the December 2022 and March 2023 transactions, no restructuring took place. No clients were lost. No bankruptcy was filed. Revenue did not change. The product did not change. The only variable was the price the board chose to set.

Perseus paid market price in December. It received a 95% discount in March. It paid market price again in June. The discount was not a valuation correction — it was a one-time departure from every other transaction in SyncPilot’s capital history, granted to one entity, with no documented justification in the public record.

A November 2025 attorney note by RA Tomanke asserts that €13 per share was actually paid by Perseus in the March 2023 transaction — a total of approximately €1.66 million rather than the €127,885 recorded in the Handelsregister. We are aware of this claim. The Handelsregister filing is the operative public record. The Tomanke note is a post-hoc claim made by a party with an interest in the litigation outcome, and neither figure has been tested against primary bank records. Both the Handelsregister and the attorney note are preserved in the Evidence Room (Appendix D). What neither version resolves is why a transaction involving this buyer, at this moment, carries any ambiguity in its documentation at all.

The identity of the buyers who received this discount — and what they had done in the three years before acquiring it — determines whether the anomaly was a commercial negotiating outcome or a reward for something else. The next chapter answers that question.

Chapter Four

The Buyers Who Got the Discount Were Released from Pre-Trial Detention Twenty-Two Months Earlier

The illusion: H.G. Perseus is a credentialed professional services entity whose principals have been cleared.

The presiding judge at the Landgericht Augsburg, at the conclusion of forty-four trial days, described further proceedings as “a waste of resources.” That characterisation — made from the bench, about a case involving 123 defendants and estimated losses of €1 billion — is not an assessment of defendant innocence. It is an assessment of prosecutorial economics. The distinction matters.

H.G. stands for Hunstein and Goerg. The two Munich lawyers who co-own the holding company named it after themselves.

Martin Hunstein (born 10 June 1971, Munich) is managing director and 50% shareholder of H.G. Perseus GmbH StBG. He is a partner at GHR Goerg Hunstein Rechtsanwälte Steuerberater, Munich. He holds a 3.51% beneficial stake in SyncPilot — 195,844 shares — through Perseus Innovations GmbH and ZKP Innovations Holding GmbH.

Both were defendants in the Goldfinger tax fraud prosecution. Both were held in pre-trial detention.

The Judge Called Continued Proceedings “A Waste of Resources.” This Is Not an Acquittal.

The “Goldfinger” scheme exploited precious metals trades to generate fictitious tax losses, artificially reducing taxable income. Prosecutors estimated total losses of approximately €1 billion — one of the largest tax fraud investigations in German history.

In January 2018, 800 officers and 30 prosecutors raided more than 200 premises across Germany, Austria, and Switzerland. Of 123 suspects investigated, seven were held in pre-trial detention, including Hunstein and Goerg. Their law firm, AFR Aigner Fischer Rechtsanwälte, was dissolved as a direct consequence of the investigation.

The Landgericht Augsburg heard the case across forty-four trial days between November 2019 and January 2021. In January 2021, proceedings were dismissed under §153 II StPO — a prosecutorial decision not to continue, not a verdict on the merits. This is not exoneration. It does not restore the defendants to a clean compliance record; it records that the state chose not to pursue a conviction to trial. Both Hunstein and Goerg subsequently filed compensation claims against the state — reportedly in the millions of euros. That response to a §153 II dismissal is not consistent with an expectation of full vindication.

Twenty-two months after their release from pre-trial detention, Hunstein and Goerg’s entity acquired 127,885 SyncPilot shares at a 95% discount.

Whether SyncPilot’s Board Asked

The question the record does not answer is whether Bleicher’s board conducted any background check before granting the discount. If Bleicher was aware of the Goldfinger prosecution before the March 2023 transaction, the pricing decision is more difficult to explain as commercially motivated. If he was not aware, the due diligence failure is the finding. Either way, the question belongs in any compliance review of this transaction.

Under standard KYC protocols, a lender or institutional counterparty extending financing to an entity would conduct a background check on its beneficial owners. H.G. Perseus’s principals were among 123 suspects in one of Germany’s largest tax fraud investigations, had been detained, and had their law firm dissolved. The question is not whether SyncPilot was aware of this — it is whether SyncPilot asked.

Chapter Five

The Lawyer Who Advised the Investor Co-Owned the Entity That Got the Discount

The illusion: SyncPilot’s investor relationships are legitimate, properly structured, and independently represented.

The lawyer who advised LVS GmbH on its €18.75 million investment agreement with SyncPilot is Martin Hunstein. Hunstein is also the managing director and 50% owner of H.G. Perseus GmbH — the entity that, twenty-two months after LVS signed the agreement, acquired 127,885 SyncPilot shares at €1.00 each while every other investor paid €18–21. LVS paid €6.15 million across four tranches. It received zero shares. Hunstein drafted the contract under which those payments were made. His company received a 95% discount from the same company in the same period. The board that approved the LVS terms and the board that approved the Perseus discount are the same board.

One Lawyer, Three Roles, One Beneficiary

Hunstein occupied three concurrent roles in this transaction ecosystem:

Legal advisor to LVS. He drafted the Cooperation and Call Option Agreement under which LVS paid €6.15 million. He prepared the modification agreement. He was the lawyer the investor trusted to document and protect its rights.

Managing director of H.G. Perseus. Twenty-two months after LVS signed, his entity acquired 127,885 SyncPilot shares at €1.00 — a 95% discount to the prevailing price.

Beneficiary of the insider pricing. The discount that Perseus received — and that Hunstein’s ownership stake in Perseus entitled him to — was approved by the same board whose capital decisions governed the LVS agreement.

We believe the simultaneous occupation of these roles constitutes an undisclosed conflict of interest in both transactions. Hunstein was positioned to know — as SyncPilot’s legal representative to LVS — that LVS’s investment had not been converted into equity. He was simultaneously positioned to benefit from the company’s ongoing capital decisions. The two facts are not separable.

Three years after the final tranche was paid, and one year after Perseus had acquired SyncPilot shares at €1.00, Goerg reached out to LVS, acknowledged that €6.15 million had been received by SyncPilot, and sought to re-engage the investment relationship. No restitution was offered.

On 3 May 2025, LVS’s principal told a lawyer: “The contracts are from Hunstein who was supposed to finalize them. He kept delaying.”

We regard the April 2024 Goerg communication as the operative acknowledgement in this matter. A party connected to the original transaction — co-owner of the entity that received the insider discount, partner of the lawyer who drafted the investor’s agreement — confirmed that the funds were received and that no shares, no repayment, and no conversion had occurred in the intervening three years. That acknowledgement is now more than two years old.

If the Contract Is Void, SyncPilot’s Liability Is Not Reduced — It Is Clarified

The LVS Cooperation and Call Option Agreement was, on our review, never notarised. Under German law, share option agreements of this kind are subject to notarisation requirements for enforceability. If the contract is void on this basis, LVS’s €6.15 million was transferred on a defective legal instrument. SyncPilot’s liability does not disappear — it may be reconfigured under §812 BGB (unjust enrichment), which creates a restitution obligation for funds received without valid legal basis. This interpretation requires legal review; we flag it here as a finding that materially changes the nature of the liability, not merely its size.

SyncPilot carries this potential obligation undisclosed on any balance sheet available to us.

The €6.15 Million Question: What a Forensic Audit Would Find

Franz-Xaver Bleicher — SyncPilot’s sole managing director and largest beneficial owner — controlled the allocation of all incoming funds during April–August 2021. The following facts are documented:

The money entered SyncPilot. All four tranches are confirmed by bank transfer records.

No shares were issued. The Handelsregister shows no capital event corresponding to the LVS investment.

During the same period, Bleicher acquired residential property at James-Cook-Straße 25, 86156 Augsburg, and bought out a co-owner’s stake in the BOSYNC holding structure. These acquisitions are documented. Their funding source is not.

The claim is not that Bleicher diverted the funds. The claim is that the overlap of timing — €6.15 million entering the company in the same months that Bleicher made significant personal acquisitions — combined with the absence of any forensic audit of SyncPilot’s cash flows during this period, means the central question has not been answered. Under §266 StGB (Untreue), a managing director who diverts corporate funds for personal use commits a criminal breach of fiduciary duty. Under §263 StGB (Betrug), funds solicited under false representations constitute fraud. Whether either threshold is crossed here depends on what a forensic audit of the April–August 2021 period would find.

No such audit has been conducted. No independent party has reviewed the cash flows. The managing director who controlled the allocation of those funds continues to control the company, its board, and all share pricing decisions today.

Questions for Franz-Xaver Bleicher and SyncPilot management:

Conclusion

The Record Does Not Require Management’s Cooperation to Speak for Itself

The public story of SyncPilot is a German software company trusted by the state, validated by institutional investors, and backed by a Swiss asset manager. 55 clients. 84% public sector revenue. An SAP partnership. A Pictet credit facility. A €128 million valuation.

The documentary record tells a different story.

Every share price in this company’s history was set by the board its founder controls. The company has never been independently valued. Its equity routes entirely through 26 holding entities across six jurisdictions. Its largest single investor received zero shares for €6.15 million, under a contract drafted by the lawyer whose own entity received a 95% discount from the same company in the same investment period. That lawyer was detained in pre-trial custody in a €1 billion tax fraud investigation twenty-two months before the discount was granted. The managing director acquired personal property during the months investor funds were flowing into the company. Three years later, his partner acknowledged in writing that the €6.15 million had been received and that nothing had been delivered.

Eighteen months after the company’s own transaction record implied a valuation of €5.6 million, its managing director presented a €250 million valuation to investors. Pictet provided debt financing on that basis. Hogan Lovells, with ten professionals, advised.

The arithmetic table — €5.6 million to €250 million in eighteen months, using the same board’s own numbers — does not require interpretation. It requires an explanation.

That explanation is in Pictet’s credit file. It exists. It is producible. The Perseus anomaly is timestamped in the Handelsregister. The LVS dispute is in active litigation. The control structure is registered. None of this required a confidential source.

We have asked management to respond to the specific questions posed in this report. The record does not require their cooperation to speak for itself.
